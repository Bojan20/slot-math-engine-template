**TL;DR** — The 2025-2026 baseline for online-slot RNG certification is TestU01 BigCrush (160 tests, p-value ∈ [0.001, 0.999], ~3–6 h wall-clock) plus PractRand to at least 4 TB as a pre-filter. NIST SP 800-22 is explicitly disclaimed for cryptographic RNG assessment but still referenced in some gaming frameworks. Jurisdictions converge on “cryptographically strong” PRNGs with periodic entropy reseeding; hardware RNGs require live statistical monitoring. Post-quantum implications for gaming PRNGs remain unregulated, though symmetric stream ciphers (ChaCha, AES-CTR) are considered Grover-resistant.---### Key Findings- **TestU01 BigCrush exact pass criteria** — BigCrush comprises 160 statistical tests (L’Ecuyer & Simard, 2007). TestU01 flags any p-value outside **[0.001, 0.999]** as a failure in its summary output (arXiv, 2024; HAL, 2016). However, L’Ecuyer’s guidance notes a *clear* failure at **p < 10⁻¹⁰** or **p > 1 − 10⁻¹⁰**, with the range **[10⁻¹⁰, 10⁻⁴)** deemed merely “suspicious” (arXiv, 2017). Because ~160 independent tests are run, even an ideal RNG has roughly a **27 % chance** of producing at least one suspicious p-value (arXiv, 2025), so isolated single-test failures are statistically expected; systematic repeats indicate real bias.- **PractRand sample-size baseline** — PractRand has no fixed “slot” mandate but defaults to a **32 TB** maximum (PractRand man page). The LXM authors treat **4 TB** as the informal threshold at which an algorithm is “worthy of final testing with BigCrush” (ACM TOG / Vigna, 2021). In practice, gaming labs use PractRand as a fast pre-filter; passing 4–32 TB is the de-facto 2025 standard for high-volume slot RNGs.- **NIST SP 800-22 status** — NIST decided in **April 2022** to revise SP 800-22 to add “stronger wording … rejecting its use for assessing cryptographic random number generators” (NIST; SSR 2022 slides). It is not formally deprecated for *all* purposes, but Saarinen’s public comment to NIST concluded it is “clearly obsolete, possibly harmful” for crypto. Gaming jurisdictions (e.g., Denmark’s Spillemyndigheden, IAGR MJTF) still list NIST SP 800-22 or Dieharder as acceptable suites, though GLI-19 v3.0 does not mandate any specific suite.- **Dieharder relevance** — Dieharder remains installable and cited by Pennsylvania regulations (95 % confidence) and Denmark, but the research consensus treats it as secondary to TestU01/PractRand. Sys et al. (2022) published corrections to the Dieharder battery, underscoring its maintenance mode.- **BigCrush wall-clock budget** — On modern high-end CPUs: **MT19937 ≈ 3 h**, **Philox4×32-10 ≈ 3.3 h**, **XORWOW ≈ 5.3 h** (NVIDIA cuRAND testing docs). PCG64 passed **32 TB of PractRand in ~157 h** (~6.5 days) and BigCrush in roughly comparable CPU time to MT19937 (PCG-Random blog, 2017). Xoshiro256** passes BigCrush but requires ~4 h.- **Battery hierarchy** — SmallCrush = 15 tests, minutes. Crush = 144 tests, ~20 min. BigCrush = 160 tests, hours. **Rabbit / Alphabit** are bit-oriented batteries for smaller data sets (≈1 GB). Gaming labs overwhelmingly require BigCrush for final certification.- **Jurisdiction-specific expectations**  - **UKGC RTS 7** — “Acceptably random” demonstrated by “statistical analysis using generally accepted tests.” No fixed suite or sample size; labs choose (UKGC, 2021).  - **MGA** — Third Schedule Regulation 25 requires RNG output be “randomly generated, passing appropriate statistical tests of randomness,” plus Schneier unpredictability criteria. An **EU/EEA-based lab certificate** is mandatory (MGA Approval of New Games form).  - **GLI-19 v3.0** — Requires the RNG be **“cryptographically strong”** (resistant to direct cryptanalytic, known-input, and state-compromise-extension attacks). Statistical tests evaluated at a **99 % confidence level**; labs choose tests case-by-case from a list including Chi-square, Overlaps, Coupon Collector’s, Runs, Serial Correlation, etc. (GLI-19, 2024).  - **AGCO Standard 4.05 / Ontario** — Games and RNGs must be certified by an AGCO-registered ITL; prior testing from other jurisdictions may be considered if aligned with AGCO Standards (AGCO, 2022). GLI is a registered ITL.  - **Pennsylvania** — 95 % confidence level; explicit list of 15+ tests (Chi-square, Equi-distribution, Gap, Poker, Permutation, Runs, Serial Correlation, etc.) (PA Code, 2021).- **Hardware RNG vs PRNG-with-entropy-reseed** — GLI-19 §3.3.3 mandates that **hardware RNGs** have **dynamic output monitoring** via statistical testing that **disables gameplay on malfunction**. For software PRNGs, GLI-19 §3.3.2(c) requires **periodic state modification through external entropy** to limit state-compromise extension attacks. Pure PRNGs without entropy reseeding are non-compliant under GLI-19’s cryptographic-strength requirement.- **Threshold for “failing” a single sub-test** — No jurisdiction explicitly defines this numerically beyond the lab’s chosen confidence level. In practice, labs apply TestU01’s [0.001, 0.999] window or PractRand’s “fail” at **p ∉ [10⁻¹¹, 1 − 10⁻¹¹]** (PMC, 2023). A single isolated failure in BigCrush is usually retested; systematic failures on repeats are certification-killers.- **Post-quantum / stream-cipher PRNGs in gaming** — No gaming regulator currently mandates post-quantum RNG standards. Symmetric stream-cipher-based PRNGs (ChaCha, AES-CTR, Salsa20) are generally regarded as **quantum-resistant against Grover’s algorithm** (quadratic speedup, mitigated by doubling key/state size). Shor’s algorithm does not break symmetric primitives. NIST’s PQC standards (ML-KEM, ML-DSA) do not replace PRNGs; they rely on them. The consensus: existing CSPRNGs (ChaCha20, AES-CTR-DRBG) are adequate for gaming through the 2025-2030 horizon unless a jurisdiction explicitly adopts QRNG mandates.---### Contradictions / Open Questions1. **TestU01 p-value interpretation** — The library summary uses [0.001, 0.999], but L’Ecuyer’s papers describe <10⁻¹⁰ as “clear failure.” Gaming labs do not uniformly document which threshold they apply for certification passes.2. **NIST SP 800-22 in gaming** — NIST disclaims it for crypto; Denmark and IAGR still accept it. This creates a gap between cryptographic best-practice and regulatory checklists.3. **PractRand “how much is enough”** — There is no regulator-mandated TB count. The 4 TB academic threshold is influential but arbitrary from a compliance-law perspective.---### Practical Implications for Boki / Cortex- **For Cortex slot RNG**: Target **PractRand to 4 TB minimum** (32 TB ideal) as an internal gate, then **TestU01 BigCrush** for certification artifacts. Budget ~4–6 CPU-hours per BigCrush run.- **Algorithm choice**: PCG64, Xoshiro256**, and Philox4×32-10 all pass BigCrush.
